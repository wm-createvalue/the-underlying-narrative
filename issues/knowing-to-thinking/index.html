Issue 007 · The Value Shift

From Knowing
to Thinking

What AI Is Doing to the Value of Expertise

For a century, knowing things was the path to value. Expertise meant mastery of a body of knowledge and the ability to apply it reliably. AI just made that cheap. The question is what comes next — and the answer is older than you think.

Aristotle · 350 BC
Isaiah Berlin · 1953
Marshall McLuhan · 1964
Hannah Arendt · 1958
Chapter I · The Promise

The 20th century made
one enormous bet.

The bet was this: that the path to individual and organisational value ran through the accumulation and reliable application of knowledge. Get educated. Accumulate expertise. Apply it consistently. Be depended upon for what you know.

This was not irrational. For most of the 20th century, knowledge was genuinely scarce. Information was hard to find, expensive to store, and slow to transmit. The person who knew things — the doctor, the lawyer, the consultant, the specialist — had something others could not easily get elsewhere. Their value was structural. It did not depend on how interesting they were. It depended on what they knew that you did not.

Entire systems were built around this bet. Universities credentialled it. Professional bodies protected it. Organisations were structured around it — layers of management existed largely to aggregate and transmit knowledge upward and decisions downward. The knowledge hierarchy was the value hierarchy.

“The most important thing is not to stop questioning. Curiosity has its own reason for existing.”

— Albert Einstein, 1955

Two things have happened simultaneously to undermine this bet. First, the internet made information nearly free and universally accessible. Second, AI made the reliable application of that information cheap. The structural advantage of knowing things has been eroded — and for many types of knowledge work, it has been eliminated entirely.

This is not a crisis. It is a transition. And transitions of this kind have happened before — each time repricing human capability in ways that seemed catastrophic to those holding the old skills and obvious in retrospect to everyone else.

Chapter II · The Pattern

Every media shift
reprices human capability.

McLuhan’s insight was that new media do not just deliver content differently — they restructure human perception and social organisation. The capability that was scarce before the shift becomes abundant after it. This has happened four times in the last 600 years.

1440s · The Printing Press
Memorisation becomes cheap. Analysis becomes valuable.
Before Gutenberg, the scholar who had memorised vast bodies of text was genuinely scarce and genuinely valuable. The printing press made text abundant and reproducible. Within a century, the value of pure memorisation had collapsed — and the value of the person who could analyse, interpret, and argue from texts had risen sharply. The Reformation was partly a knowledge revolution: ordinary people could now read scripture themselves and form their own judgments.
1760s · The Industrial Revolution
Craft knowledge becomes cheap. System design becomes valuable.
The skilled craftsman — the weaver, the blacksmith, the clockmaker — held value in tacit knowledge of materials, tools, and process that took years to acquire. Industrial machinery encoded much of that knowledge into mechanical systems. What it could not encode was the ability to design, manage, and improve those systems. The engineer and the manager replaced the master craftsman as the valued human in the production chain.
1990s · The Internet
Information access becomes cheap. Curation and judgment become valuable.
The encyclopedia salesman, the reference librarian, the specialist who knew where to find obscure information — these roles were structurally undermined when every fact became a search query away. What rose in value was the ability to navigate, evaluate, and synthesise the flood of information. The analyst, the editor, the strategist — people who could make sense of abundance rather than just provide access to it.
2020s · Artificial Intelligence
Knowledge application becomes cheap. Original thinking becomes valuable.
AI does not just store and retrieve information. It applies it — drafting, analysing, summarising, coding, diagnosing, predicting. The reliable application of existing knowledge to known problem types, which was the core activity of the 20th-century knowledge worker, is now being automated at scale. What it cannot do is generate genuine insight, exercise judgment in genuinely novel situations, or think in ways that have not been thought before. That capability — which Aristotle named phronesis 2,400 years ago — is now the scarce resource.
Chapter III · The Framework

Aristotle named this problem
2,400 years ago.

In the Nicomachean Ethics, Aristotle distinguished three types of knowledge. The distinction was not academic — he was trying to understand what it meant to live and act well. His categories map onto the AI transition with unsettling precision.

Episteme
Scientific knowledge · Knowing that
Knowledge of fixed, demonstrable truths. The kind that can be written down, transmitted, and verified. Water boils at 100°C. The Battle of Hastings was in 1066. Episteme is propositional — it consists of statements that are either true or false.
Can do this: retrieves, stores, and applies epistemic knowledge near-perfectly at essentially zero marginal cost.
Techne
Craft knowledge · Knowing how
Knowledge of how to make or do things. The surgeon’s skill, the programmer’s facility with code, the architect’s spatial reasoning. Techne is procedural — it can in principle be reduced to rules and learned patterns of action.
Can do this increasingly: code generation, medical imaging analysis, legal document drafting. The boundary is moving rapidly.
Phronesis
Practical wisdom · Knowing what matters
The capacity to discern the right course of action in particular, uncertain, morally complex situations. Phronesis cannot be reduced to rules — it is precisely the form of knowledge required when rules run out. It involves judgment, experience, values, and the ability to read a specific human situation in its full complexity.
Cannot do this: phronesis requires genuine uncertainty, genuine values, and genuine skin in the game. No current or near-future AI has any of these.

“It is the mark of an educated mind to be able to entertain a thought without accepting it.”

— Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics

The 20th century organisation rewarded episteme and techne above phronesis — because episteme and techne were scarce and phronesis was assumed. AI has separated them. When episteme and techne can be purchased cheaply, phronesis stands alone as the irreducible human contribution.

Chapter IV · Berlin’s Distinction

The fox and the hedgehog.
Only one is being automated.

Isaiah Berlin’s 1953 essay drew on a fragment from the Greek poet Archilochus: “The fox knows many things; the hedgehog knows one big thing.” Berlin used it to distinguish two fundamental types of intellectual personality. The distinction has acquired a new urgency in the age of AI.

🦔
The Hedgehog
Knows one big thing · Depth · Consistency
The hedgehog relates everything to a single central organising idea. Hedgehogs are consistent, principled, and often brilliant within their domain — applying a single clarifying lens with extraordinary depth and rigour.
The hedgehog is being automated. AI is the ultimate hedgehog at scale — it applies any known framework to any known problem type with exhaustive consistency. The value of hedgehog thinking that stays within established frameworks is declining rapidly.
AI is a perfect hedgehog. It applies known frameworks exhaustively.
versus
🦊
The Fox
Knows many things · Breadth · Surprise
The fox pursues many different ends, drawing on a wide range of experience, making unexpected connections across domains, resisting the pull of any single organising framework. They find the angle nobody else saw because nobody else was standing in the same place.
The fox’s value is rising. The ability to connect across domains, to bring an unexpected perspective from outside the established framework, to notice what the hedgehog’s single lens misses — this is precisely what AI cannot do.
The fox’s cross-domain thinking is the rising scarce resource.

What is being devalued is hedgehog thinking alone — the specialist who applies known frameworks to known problems without ever stepping outside them. That is the job AI is taking.

Chapter V · Arendt’s Argument

What it actually means
to be interesting.

Hannah Arendt’s The Human Condition (1958) distinguished three fundamental human activities. Labour — the biological maintenance of life. Work — the fabrication of durable objects and systems. And action — the uniquely human capacity to begin something new, to appear in the world in a way that changes it.

Action is inherently unpredictable, inherently relational, and inherently revelatory of who the person is. It is what makes someone genuinely present in the world — not their credentials, not their knowledge base, but their capacity to initiate something that could not have been predicted.

“Action, the only activity that goes on directly between men without the intermediary of things or matter, corresponds to the human condition of plurality.”

— Hannah Arendt, The Human Condition, 1958

This is what Socrates was. He claimed to know nothing. He had no expertise to offer in the conventional sense. His entire value was in his capacity to initiate — to ask the question that revealed the inadequacy of the current answer. Athens executed him for it, which tells you something about how threatening genuine intellectual action is to established systems.

The person who is interesting in this sense is not the opposite of knowledgeable. They are typically deeply knowledgeable in at least one area. But they use that knowledge as a launching pad for genuine inquiry rather than as a destination. Their expertise enables their action rather than substituting for it.

Chapter VI · So What Do You Do?

Moving from the knowing mode
to the thinking mode.

These are specific practices — applicable to individuals building their own intellectual capacity and to leaders thinking about how to build it in their organisations and teams.

1
For individuals & leaders
Cultivate questions, not just answers
A knowledgeable person is valued for the quality of their answers. A thinking person is valued for the quality of their questions — the ones that reveal why the current answer is insufficient. Deliberately practise asking the question one level deeper than the one being discussed. The question that makes the room go quiet is worth more than the answer everyone already expected.
2
For individuals
Build across domains deliberately
Fox thinking requires genuine exposure to multiple domains — not surface familiarity, but real enough engagement to understand how each domain structures its problems and what counts as evidence. The connections between domains are where genuinely novel thinking lives. Choose one domain adjacent to your primary expertise and engage with it seriously for twelve months. Not to become an expert — to see your primary domain from the outside.
3
For leaders & organisations
Reward judgment, not just execution
Most organisations measure and reward execution — the reliable completion of defined tasks. This made sense when execution was the scarce human contribution. It makes less sense when AI executes reliably at lower cost. Identify the moments in your organisation where genuine judgment is exercised — where someone reads an ambiguous situation and makes a call that could not have been derived from a rulebook. Ask whether your measurement systems recognise those moments, or whether they are invisible in your metrics.
4
For individuals & leaders
Use AI to amplify thinking, not replace it
The person who uses AI to avoid thinking is building a skill deficit. The person who uses AI to extend their thinking is building a capability advantage. The distinction is specific: use AI for the episteme and techne work — research, synthesis, drafting, pattern recognition — and use the time freed for the phronesis work that AI cannot do: the judgment calls, the novel connections, the questions that change the frame.
