Issue 004 · Business Strategy

The Strategy
Paradox

Why most organisations plan brilliantly and strategise barely at all

Richard Rumelt — one of the field's most rigorous thinkers — estimates that roughly 90% of what is called "strategy" in organisations is not strategy at all. It is goals dressed in strategic language, planning mistaken for positioning, and aspiration substituting for choice. AI is accelerating every one of these failures while making the outputs look more impressive than ever.

"A strategy is a coherent set of analyses, concepts, policies, arguments, and actions that respond to a high-stakes challenge."

— Richard Rumelt, Good Strategy Bad Strategy (2011)

1 Diagnosis
What is actually going on here? What is the challenge, precisely? What forces are at work?
2 Guiding Policy
How will we approach the challenge? What is the overall method of dealing with it?
3 Coherent Actions
What specific, coordinated actions will implement the guiding policy? Do they reinforce each other?
Chapter I · The Foundation

Strategy vs planning.
The clearest distinction in the field.

The confusion between these two activities is not semantic. It is structurally incentivised: planning produces visible outputs — documents, budgets, timelines — that organisations can point to as evidence of strategic activity. Strategy produces choices and trade-offs, which are invisible, uncomfortable, and politically costly. Most organisations choose the visible over the valuable.

Planning
Operational · Convergent · Allocates resources · Reduces uncertainty
Core question: How do we execute the direction we've chosen?
Time horizon: Known future, extrapolated from present conditions
Uncertainty: Minimises it through detailed specification of actions
Political nature: Relatively comfortable — allocates resources, assigns responsibility
Output: Budget, roadmap, OKRs, milestones, resource plans
Where AI helps enormously: Scenario modelling, forecasting, optimisation, variance analysis
Strategy
Positional · Divergent · Makes choices · Accepts uncertainty
Core question: Where will we compete, how will we win, and what will we not do?
Time horizon: Uncertain future, shaped by judgment and insight about how value is created
Uncertainty: Accepts it as the condition under which the choice is made — cannot be eliminated
Political nature: Deeply uncomfortable — requires saying no, making trade-offs, being wrong publicly
Output: A clear integrated set of choices about where and how to win
Where AI helps modestly: Research, pattern recognition, devil's advocate — not the choice itself
Chapter II · The Modern Framework

Playing to Win:
the clearest modern account of what strategy actually is.

Roger Martin and A.G. Lafley's "Playing to Win" (2014) is the most practically rigorous strategy framework of the modern era. It defines strategy as an integrated cascade of five choices — each of which must be coherent with all the others. The test of whether you have a strategy is simple: can you answer all five questions specifically and show how they reinforce each other? Most organisations cannot.

1
What is our winning aspiration? The purpose · Not a goal — a theory of winning
▼

The first question is not "what do we want to achieve?" It is "what does winning look like for us — and for whom?" This is not a revenue target or a market share goal. It is a statement of the kind of value you intend to create, for which customers, and why your doing so constitutes a genuinely superior outcome for them and for you.

The distinction matters because goals are internally oriented (we want to grow 20%) while a winning aspiration is externally oriented (we intend to be the essential partner for X type of customer facing Y type of problem). Goals tell you if you're succeeding. An aspiration tells you what succeeding means.

A weak winning aspiration sounds like: "We will be a leading provider of enterprise software." A strong one sounds like: "We will make the skilled trades workforce as productive as knowledge workers — starting with the scheduling and dispatch problem that costs service businesses 30% of their revenue."

Substituting a revenue target or market share goal for a genuine aspiration. A goal tells you whether you've won. An aspiration tells you what you're playing for.
AI can help you pressure-test the aspiration against market evidence and customer research — but cannot generate a genuine winning aspiration. That requires a view about how value will be created in the future, which is a judgment call.
2
Where will we play? The arena · Defining the competitive space explicitly
▼

"Where to play" is a choice about the competitive arenas in which your strategy will operate: which customers, which geographies, which channels, which product categories, which stages of the value chain. The choice is as much about what you will not do as what you will.

Martin's consistent finding across decades of strategic consulting: most leadership teams can answer "where to play" at a high level (enterprise software, mid-market healthcare, European retail) but struggle to specify the choice precisely enough to be useful. "Mid-market" is not a where-to-play choice. "Companies with 200–2,000 employees in professional services that are currently managing their operations in spreadsheets" is a where-to-play choice. The specificity is what makes it actionable and what makes it a genuine constraint.

The where-to-play choice must also specify, explicitly, where you will not play — which segments, customer types, or geographies you are actively choosing to exit or ignore. Without this, "where to play" is not a strategic choice, it is a description of current activity.

Defining the playing field so broadly that it provides no constraint. "Global enterprise customers" is not a where-to-play choice — it describes almost every B2B company's aspiration.
Strong AI use case: market sizing, customer segmentation, competitive density mapping. AI can significantly improve the information quality that informs where-to-play choices — it cannot make the trade-off between options.
3
How will we win? The value proposition · The hardest question in the cascade
▼

This is where most strategies fail. Martin's observation: organisations can usually answer where they play, but they leave "how to win" as a generic aspiration — "by delivering superior value," "by being the most customer-centric," "by leveraging our deep domain expertise." These are not how-to-win statements. They are descriptions of any competent company's aspiration.

A genuine how-to-win statement describes the specific advantage you have or intend to build in your chosen competitive arena — and that advantage must be either a cost advantage (you do something at lower cost than rivals can) or a differentiation advantage (you do something that customers value that rivals cannot or do not do). There is no third category.

Porter's insight remains correct here: the source of competitive advantage is always either lower cost or unique value — and these require different operational models, cultures, and investment patterns. A how-to-win statement that does not specify which of these you are pursuing, and why you can sustain it, is not a strategy.

Leaving "how to win" vague enough that it is compatible with any set of resource allocation decisions. If your how-to-win statement doesn't tell you what to say no to, it isn't one.
Limited but real: AI can analyse competitor positioning and customer value drivers to identify underserved needs or cost structure gaps. It cannot tell you whether you have the will, judgment, and resources to build a specific advantage.
4
What capabilities must we have? The activity system · What you must be genuinely excellent at
▼

Given the where-to-play and how-to-win choices, what capabilities are required to execute them? This is where strategy connects to operations. Capabilities are the things an organisation must be genuinely excellent at — not simply competent, but distinctively capable — to win in the chosen arena.

The critical insight from Hamel and Prahalad's earlier work on core competencies: capabilities are not the same as resources or activities. A capability is a bundle of skills, processes, systems, and culture that produces a distinctive outcome. Amazon's capability is not "warehouses" — it is the integrated system of inventory prediction, logistics optimisation, and last-mile delivery that produces next-day delivery economics at scale. That capability took 20 years and $100 billion to build and is essentially impossible to replicate quickly.

The capabilities question forces a crucial internal audit: of the capabilities required by our strategy, which do we already have? Which do we need to build? Which are we currently investing in that our strategy does not require?

Listing aspirational capabilities rather than specific ones. "Data-driven decision making" and "talent excellence" are not capabilities — they are descriptions of what every organisation claims to value.
Useful: AI can benchmark capabilities against competitors, identify capability gaps through job posting analysis and patent filings, and model build-vs-buy-vs-partner decisions for specific capability investments.
5
What management systems are required? The enablers · Structure, incentives, processes, culture
▼

The fifth choice asks: what organisational systems must exist to build and sustain the required capabilities? This is where Chandler's foundational insight — structure follows strategy — becomes operational. The required systems include organisational structure, measurement and reporting, incentive design, talent systems, and the cultural norms that determine how people behave when no one is watching.

This is the most commonly neglected level of the cascade. Organisations articulate a strategy, identify required capabilities, and then attempt to execute through existing management systems designed for a different strategy. The result is strategy on paper and a different set of behaviours on the ground.

The most visible version: a company articulates a long-term differentiation strategy while measuring and rewarding quarterly margin performance. The incentive system overrides the strategy every quarter. No amount of strategy communication changes behaviour when the measurement system says something different.

Attempting to implement a new strategy through management systems designed for the previous one. The systems will win. Strategy without aligned systems is a document.
Limited: AI can model organisational network analysis and flag incentive misalignments at scale. But redesigning incentive systems and culture requires human judgment about values and what kind of organisation you are trying to build.
Chapter III · Rumelt's Contribution

Good Strategy / Bad Strategy:
the diagnosis most organisations refuse to make.

Rumelt's framework is deceptively simple. Good strategy has a kernel — three elements that must all be present. Bad strategy has four characteristic failure modes that are almost universal in large organisations. The uncomfortable truth: most corporate strategy documents fail Rumelt's test entirely.

Kernel element 1
Diagnosis
"What is actually going on here?"
A good diagnosis simplifies the often overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying certain aspects of the situation as critical. It names the challenge. Not the general challenge ("competitive markets are tough") but the specific challenge your organisation faces right now that this strategy must address. Without a precise diagnosis, the guiding policy has nothing to respond to.
Does your strategy name a specific challenge? Or does it describe a general aspiration?
Kernel element 2
Guiding Policy
"How will we approach this challenge?"
The guiding policy is the overall approach chosen to deal with the obstacles identified in the diagnosis. It channels action in certain directions without defining exactly what shall be done. Crucially, a guiding policy constrains action — it rules out some responses and focuses resources on others. A policy that is compatible with everything is a policy that guides nothing.
Does your guiding policy rule anything out? If not, it isn't one.
Kernel element 3
Coherent Actions
"What specifically will we do — and do they reinforce each other?"
Good strategy is not a list of initiatives — it is a set of coordinated actions that reinforce each other and collectively implement the guiding policy. The coherence is what creates leverage. Southwest Airlines' low-cost strategy works not because of any single action (no seat assignments, no meals, short-haul focus, single aircraft type) but because all of those actions reinforce each other to produce a cost structure no rival can match without abandoning their own strategy.
Do your strategic actions reinforce each other? Or is your strategy a list of good things to do?

The four hallmarks of bad strategy.

Rumelt's diagnosis of bad strategy is specific and damning. These are not edge cases — they describe the majority of strategy documents produced by large organisations.

Fluff
Restatement of the obvious combined with buzzwords that creates the appearance of high-level thinking. "Our strategy is to leverage our core competencies to deliver synergistic value across our ecosystem of partners." This sentence means nothing. It constrains nothing. It guides nothing. It is strategic-sounding noise.
Failure to face the challenge
Bad strategy skips the diagnosis. It jumps directly to goals without naming what obstacle the strategy must overcome. If the strategy does not articulate why your current position is insufficient and what specifically makes it hard to improve, it has no foundation to build from.
Mistaking goals for strategy
"We will grow revenue 25% and improve NPS by 15 points." This is a wish list, not a strategy. Goals describe a desired outcome. Strategy describes how you will achieve an outcome that would otherwise not be achieved — what specific choices, trade-offs, and actions will get you there when simply wanting it is not enough.
Bad strategic objectives
Strategic objectives that are either too vague to act on ("be more customer-centric") or too operationally specific to constitute strategy ("increase sales team headcount by 30%"). Good strategic objectives sit in between: specific enough to guide action, high-level enough to allow the people doing the work to exercise judgment about how to pursue them.
Chapter IV · The Intellectual History

How strategic thought evolved —
and which ideas actually survived.

The frameworks that dominate strategy today were built in response to specific historical failures of earlier thinking. Understanding the evolution explains why the field looks the way it does — and why some 1960s ideas still matter while some 1990s ones have been quietly abandoned.

1960s · Systematisation
Andrews, Ansoff and the birth of formal strategy
Kenneth Andrews · Igor Ansoff · Alfred Chandler
Andrews codified SWOT as a diagnostic. Ansoff built the first rigorous growth direction framework (the Ansoff Matrix). Chandler established that structure follows strategy — the insight that remains correct and remains routinely ignored. The problem this era created: it began the slow conflation of strategic analysis with strategic choice. Producing a thorough SWOT is not the same as deciding what to do about it.
Enduring: Chandler's structure-follows-strategy remains the most violated principle in corporate change management
1980s · The Porter Decade
Competitive strategy as economic science
Michael Porter — Harvard Business School
Porter's Five Forces (1980) gave strategists a framework to analyse industry attractiveness. His Value Chain gave them a way to identify where advantage actually lived. Most critically, his 1996 HBR paper "What Is Strategy?" drew the sharpest distinction in the field: strategy is about trade-offs. Operational effectiveness (doing the same things better than rivals) is not strategy. Strategy requires choosing to do different things — or the same things differently — in ways that create a unique and valuable position that is hard to imitate. This distinction remains the clearest definition of competitive strategy ever written.
Enduring: "Strategy is about trade-offs — choosing what NOT to do" (Porter, 1996)
1990s · The Critique and the Capabilities Turn
Mintzberg demolishes planning; Hamel and Prahalad build from inside out
Henry Mintzberg · Gary Hamel · C.K. Prahalad
Mintzberg's Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning (1994) made the argument that formal strategic planning systematically kills strategic thinking by formalising judgment out of existence. His emergent vs deliberate distinction — that realised strategy is always a blend of what was planned and what the market taught you — fundamentally changed how the best organisations run strategy processes. Hamel and Prahalad's core competencies framework argued that durable advantage lives in organisational capabilities, not market position — a necessary correction to Porter's outside-in bias.
Enduring: emergent strategy is as valid as deliberate strategy — the best processes make room for both
2011 · The Diagnostic Breakthrough
Good Strategy Bad Strategy — Rumelt names what everyone avoided naming
Richard Rumelt — UCLA Anderson
Rumelt's contribution was diagnostic rather than prescriptive. He was the first major thinker to name clearly and specifically what bad strategy looks like — and to demonstrate that most organisations produce it. The kernel framework (diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent actions) is the most rigorous minimum viable definition of strategy in the literature. His observation that 90% of "strategies" are really goal lists dressed in strategic language was controversial when published. It has proved accurate.
Why it changed the field: Before Rumelt, strategy theorists debated what good strategy looked like. Rumelt shifted the conversation to what bad strategy looks like — and gave leaders a specific, non-negotiable test to apply to their own organisations.
Enduring: the kernel test — does your strategy have a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions?
2014 · The Cascade Framework
Playing to Win — Martin and Lafley operationalise strategic choice
Roger Martin · A.G. Lafley — P&G / Rotman School
Martin's five-choice cascade (winning aspiration → where to play → how to win → capabilities → management systems) is the most practically useful strategy framework of the modern era. Its power comes from the cascade logic: each choice must be coherent with all the others, and an inconsistency at any level exposes a strategic flaw. It was developed and pressure-tested over a decade of strategic work at P&G — one of the most studied examples of sustained competitive strategy in consumer goods. The framework is not abstract; it was built from the decisions that produced Pampers' global dominance, Olay's repositioning, and Tide's sustained market leadership across fifty years.
The test Martin applies: Can you state all five choices specifically? Are they coherent with each other? Does the "how to win" choice actually specify a source of advantage — or is it a generic aspiration?
The most important modern strategy framework — built from practice, not theory
2016 · The Power Framework
7 Powers — Hamilton Helmer asks what makes advantage durable
Hamilton Helmer — Strategy Capital
Helmer's question: if competitive advantage is eventually competed away (as McGrath's transient advantage thesis suggests), what are the specific mechanisms by which some advantages last? His answer: seven structural barriers that prevent competitors from capturing the value you create — Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, and Process Power. The framework is particularly useful for technology and platform businesses where traditional Porter analysis misses the dynamics of network effects and switching costs.
Particularly valuable: Counter-Positioning explains why incumbents rationally choose not to respond to disruptors
Present · The Adaptive Imperative
When competitive advantage is transient, strategy becomes a capability
Rita McGrath · BCG Henderson Institute
McGrath's core argument: in industries with rapid technology change, the conditions that made a strategy work rarely persist long enough to justify the 3–5 year planning cycles most organisations still use. Sustainable advantage is increasingly rare. The strategic imperative shifts from find a durable position and defend it to build the organisational capability to identify, exploit, and abandon advantages faster than rivals. This does not mean strategy is dead — it means the time horizon and mechanism of strategy change. The cascade still applies. The question of how to win must be answered continuously, not once.
The strategy question becomes: how fast can we move from one winning position to the next?
Chapter V · Hamilton Helmer

7 Powers: the specific mechanisms
by which competitive advantage persists.

Helmer's framework answers the question Porter left open: exactly how does an advantage become durable? Each of the seven powers is a specific structural barrier that prevents competitors from capturing the value you create — even after they understand what you are doing.

Power 1
Scale Economies
A business in which per-unit cost declines as volume increases — giving large players a cost advantage that small players cannot overcome without first achieving scale they cannot yet afford.
Amazon fulfilment, Intel fabs, Netflix content spend
Power 2
Network Economies
The value of a service increases for all users as more users join. The incumbent benefits from an ever-widening gap — more users make the service better, making it harder for a rival to attract the critical mass needed to compete.
Visa, LinkedIn, WhatsApp, stock exchanges
Power 3
Counter-Positioning
A new business model that is superior to the incumbent's but which the incumbent rationally chooses not to adopt — because doing so would damage their existing, profitable business. The incumbent's own economics become their constraint.
Netflix vs Blockbuster, Vanguard vs active fund managers
Power 4
Switching Costs
The accumulated costs — financial, operational, or relational — that a customer faces when changing suppliers. High switching costs allow incumbents to raise prices and reduce service quality within limits without losing customers.
Salesforce CRM, SAP ERP, Bloomberg Terminal
Power 5
Branding
The durable attribution of higher value to an objectively similar offering — built through consistent delivery of a specific promise over time. True brand power is a structural barrier, not a marketing tactic. It requires decades to build and can be destroyed quickly.
Hermès, Patagonia, Apple's premium pricing
Power 6
Cornered Resource
Preferential access to a coveted resource — talent, IP, data, geography, regulation — that others cannot easily replicate. The key test: is the resource itself protected, or just the product built from it?
DeBeers diamond supply, Pixar's creative talent cluster, TSMC's process IP
Power 7
Process Power
Embedded company organisation and activity sets that enable lower costs or superior product that cannot be replicated quickly. The process must be hysteretic — built through years of learning and refinement that cannot be shortcut.
Toyota Production System, IKEA's supply chain, Zara's fast fashion cycle
The key question
Which power(s) does your strategy build?
A strategy that does not deliberately build at least one of these seven powers is a strategy that generates competitive advantage which will be competed away. The cascade (where to play / how to win) must connect to at least one power — otherwise "how to win" is temporary, not structural.
Chapter VI · The AI Reality Check

What AI actually does in strategy.
No hype. No dismissal. Plainly.

The strategy software and consulting market is saturated with AI claims. Below is an honest, framework-grounded assessment — mapped to the Playing to Win cascade and Rumelt's kernel — of where AI genuinely contributes and where it cannot, structurally, replace human judgment.

✓ Genuine value
Diagnosis support: environmental scanning at scale
Rumelt's kernel begins with diagnosis — naming the specific challenge. AI significantly improves the information quality available for diagnosis: synthesising earnings transcripts, regulatory filings, patent databases, academic research, competitor communications, and customer sentiment at a speed no human team can match. This sharpens the lens. The diagnosis itself — deciding which of the many patterns you've identified is the one your strategy must address — remains a judgment call that requires contextual understanding AI does not have.
✓ Genuine value
Where to play: market sizing and segmentation
The "where to play" choice requires understanding the size, growth, competitive density, and accessibility of different market segments. AI is genuinely excellent at this: market sizing models, customer segmentation from large datasets, competitive mapping, and regulatory analysis. It improves the quality of the information available to make the where-to-play choice — it does not make the trade-off between options, which requires a view about which opportunity fits your aspiration and capabilities.
~ Partial value
How to win: competitive positioning analysis
AI can map competitor positioning, identify underserved customer segments, and surface potential differentiation opportunities from large datasets. This is useful input into the how-to-win choice. The limitation: the how-to-win choice requires a theory about what customers will value in the future — which is a prediction about human behaviour under conditions that don't yet exist. AI trained on past behaviour will systematically underweight genuinely novel value propositions because, by definition, they have no historical precedent to learn from.
✓ Genuine value
Planning translation: scenario modelling and optimisation
Once strategic direction is set, AI dramatically accelerates the planning translation. Scenario modelling that took analyst teams weeks now takes hours. Financial sensitivity analysis, capacity planning, resource allocation optimisation — all of these are genuine AI productivity gains. The critical precondition: strategy must be set first. AI will optimise any scenario you give it. It cannot tell you which scenarios are worth optimising.
~ Partial value
Stress-testing: red-teaming strategic assumptions
A genuinely valuable but underused application: explicitly instructing AI to generate the strongest possible case against your strategy. "What would have to be true for this strategy to fail?" is a question AI can explore systematically — generating alternative scenarios, stress-testing assumption dependencies, and surfacing logical inconsistencies. The caveat: AI will miss the unknown unknowns by definition. It will explore the scenarios you ask it to explore, not the ones you haven't imagined. A useful devil's advocate, not an exhaustive one.
✗ Does not do this
Making the strategic choice
The core strategic question — where will we compete, and how will we win? — requires a judgment about an uncertain future that no AI can make. This is not a temporary limitation. It is structural: the choice requires a theory of how the world will unfold, a values-based decision about what kind of organisation you want to be, and a willingness to be accountably wrong in public. Any organisation that believes it has delegated strategy to AI has not made a strategy. It has produced a sophisticated planning document and called it strategy.
✗ Does not do this
Recommending genuinely novel strategic positions
AI is trained on past strategies and documented best practices. By definition, the strategies in the training data have already been executed by someone — they are already competed away. Strategy is about finding a position that is difficult to imitate, which means it must be, to some degree, novel. The most consequential strategic moves in business history — Southwest's point-to-point low cost model, Amazon's flywheel, Apple's platform ecosystem — were not derivable from prior industry patterns. AI trained on those patterns would have recommended incumbents' strategies.
✗ Does not do this
Building organisational commitment to strategy
Martin's fifth choice — management systems — includes the cultural alignment and leadership commitment that make a strategy real rather than documentary. This is a deeply human process: persuading a CFO that the long-term bet is worth the short-term margin pressure, building a leadership team that genuinely owns the strategic choices, creating the conditions in which middle management believes the strategy is real and will be sustained. AI accelerates document production, which often reduces commitment — the document arrives before the conversation that would have built alignment.
