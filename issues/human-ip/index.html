550 Years of Human IP
1474
Venice
Patent

The World's First Patent Law

The Venetian Senate passed the Parte Veneziana — the world's first formal patent statute — granting 10-year monopolies to inventors of "new and ingenious devices." The primary motive was economic espionage prevention: Venice's glassblowers on Murano island were forbidden to leave the republic on pain of death, and the Republic didn't want its industrial secrets walking out the door. IP law began as a state security tool, not a creator's right.

"Any person who shall build any new and ingenious device... not previously made in our dominion, shall give it to us."

10-year monopoly State-granted privilege Murano glassblowers Industrial espionage law
1710
England
Copyright

The Statute of Anne: Copyright Is Born

Before 1710, publishers (not authors) owned books in perpetuity through a guild monopoly called the Stationers' Company. The Statute of Anne shattered this: for the first time, authors gained legal ownership of their works — for 14 years, renewable once. This was revolutionary. It recognized that the mind that created a work deserved protection, not merely the machine that printed it. Every modern copyright law descends from this single Act of Parliament.

"An Act for the Encouragement of Learning" — the first copyright law placed the creator, not the printer, at the center.

14-year term Author's rights Broke publisher monopoly Foundation of all copyright
1790
United States
Patent

The US Patent System & the Founding Bargain

The US Constitution explicitly empowered Congress to grant patents "to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts." The first US Patent Act (1790) was signed by George Washington. The founding bargain was deliberate: the inventor receives a limited monopoly; in exchange, they must fully disclose their invention to the public. After the patent expires, the knowledge belongs to everyone. Thomas Jefferson, the first patent examiner, personally reviewed every application. By 1836, volume was so high that the Patent Office became its own institution.

"The patent system added the fuel of interest to the fire of genius." — Abraham Lincoln, the only US president to hold a patent.

Constitutional basis Disclosure for monopoly Jefferson as examiner Limited term bargain
1800s
Industrial Age
Trade Secret

Trade Secrets & The Rise of Industrial Espionage

The 19th century saw a new form of IP protection emerge: the trade secret. Unlike patents, trade secrets require no disclosure and last forever — as long as you can keep them. Coca-Cola's formula (1886) became the canonical example, famously never patented to avoid the mandatory disclosure. Steel mills, textile manufacturers, and chemical companies developed elaborate systems of secrecy, employee oaths, and industrial counterintelligence. Trade secrets drove the first non-compete clauses — still contested in courts today.

Coca-Cola's formula has never been patented. Instead, it has been a trade secret for over 135 years — potentially the most valuable secret in history.

No disclosure required Infinite duration Coca-Cola formula Non-compete clauses born
1886
Paris & Berne
Copyright

IP Goes Global: The Berne Convention

Victor Hugo was furious. His novels were being reprinted without permission — and without royalties — across Europe and the Americas. His advocacy helped push the 1886 Berne Convention, which established that copyright protection should be automatic (no registration needed) and should cross national borders. 181 countries are now signatories. The US held out until 1989, previously requiring formal copyright registration. Berne turned IP from a national privilege into a global human right of creators.

Victor Hugo argued that ideas cross borders freely — so protections for those ideas should too. Berne made him right, legally.

181 signatory countries Automatic protection No registration needed Victor Hugo's legacy
1970s–90s
Software Age
Digital IP

Software, Code & the IP Scramble

When software arrived, existing IP law had no idea what to do with it. Was code a "writing" (protectable by copyright) or a "machine" (patentable)? The answer, after decades of litigation, turned out to be: both. The 1976 Copyright Act and later the 1980 Computer Software Copyright Act extended protection to code. Microsoft's legal battles over Windows and IBM's patent arsenal fundamentally shaped the industry. The open-source movement — starting with GNU (1983) and Linux (1991) — pushed back, arguing that software should be a commons, not property. This battle continues.

"Software is eating the world — and IP law spent 30 years figuring out which fork and knife to use."

Code as copyrightable text Software patents Open source counter-movement GPL license invented
1994
WTO / TRIPS
Trademark

TRIPS: IP Becomes Trade Policy

The Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS, 1994) tied IP protection to international trade for the first time. Countries that wanted access to WTO trade benefits had to adopt minimum IP standards. The US pharmaceutical industry was the key driver — it wanted patent protection for drugs in developing countries. Critics argued TRIPS made life-saving medicines unaffordable. The Doha Declaration (2001) created emergency exceptions. TRIPS transformed IP from a legal system into a geopolitical weapon.

TRIPS showed that IP law was no longer just about protecting creators — it was now a lever of global economic power.

WTO membership requirement Pharma patent wars Doha Declaration carveout IP as trade weapon
2023–Now
AI Age
Digital IP

Generative AI: The IP System's Stress Test

ChatGPT, Midjourney, and Sora shattered the IP landscape. Artists discovered their work was used to train AI without consent or compensation. Authors including John Grisham and George R.R. Martin sued. The US Copyright Office ruled in 2023 that purely AI-generated work cannot be copyrighted — only works with "sufficient human authorship" qualify. Meanwhile, the question of who owns training data, model weights, and AI outputs remains unresolved in virtually every jurisdiction. The next decade of IP law will be rewritten by AI.

"The machines learned from us. Now we're trying to figure out what that means for who owns what — and no one has the answer yet."

AI training data lawsuits No copyright for pure AI output Human authorship threshold Model weights as trade secrets

The Four Pillars of IP

Every idea you protect
lives in one of these boxes.

Intellectual property law rests on four foundational systems — each with its own logic, duration, and purpose. Understanding the differences explains why Coca-Cola chose secrecy over patents, why Mickey Mouse keeps getting protected, and why your startup's brand name matters more than you think.

⚙️
Patents
20 years · Must disclose
Protects inventions and processes. The trade-off: you get an exclusive monopoly, but you must fully reveal how your invention works. After 20 years, it's public domain. Used for drugs, devices, algorithms, and manufacturing methods.
©
Copyright
Life + 70 years · Automatic
Protects original creative expression — books, music, art, code, film. Arises automatically at creation. Doesn't protect ideas, only their fixed expression. The longest and most expansive IP right, increasingly lobbied to extend further.
™
Trademarks
Indefinite · Must be used
Protects brands, logos, slogans, and sounds that identify commercial origin. Can last forever if actively used and defended. The Nike swoosh, McDonald's golden arches, and Intel's chime are all trademarks. The most "use it or lose it" IP right.
🔒
Trade Secrets
Indefinite · Must stay secret
Any confidential business information that provides competitive advantage. No registration, no disclosure — just maintain secrecy. The formula for Coca-Cola, Google's search algorithm weighting, KFC's spice blend. Loses protection the moment it leaks.
Beyond the four pillars
Specialist Forms
🎭
Moral Rights
Varies · Non-transferable
The right of creators to be credited and to object to distortion of their work — even after selling copyright. Strong in France and Europe, weak in the US.
🌱
Plant Breeders' Rights
20–25 years
A patent-like system for new plant varieties. Farmers who save and replant seeds from protected varieties can face lawsuits — a source of major agricultural controversy.
💊
Data Exclusivity
5–12 years
Pharmaceutical companies get exclusive rights to clinical trial data — preventing generics from using it to gain approval, even after the underlying patent expires.
🗺️
Geographic Indications
Indefinite
Protects products tied to a specific place: Champagne, Parmigiano-Reggiano, Darjeeling tea. A $50B+ category and the source of intense US-EU trade disputes.
III
Chapter Three
Cases That Rewrote the Rules
📜 Copyright · Supreme Court
Eldred v. Ashcroft
2003 · United States
Congress extended copyright terms by 20 years — for the 11th time in 40 years — largely to protect Disney's Mickey Mouse. Eric Eldred, who published public domain works online, challenged it. The Supreme Court upheld the extension 7–2. Justice Breyer dissented: the extension served "no constitutional purpose." Critics dubbed it the "Mickey Mouse Protection Act."
⚡ Impact: Set precedent that Congress can extend copyright terms indefinitely, effectively ending the public domain for modern works.
⚙️ Patent · Supreme Court
Diamond v. Chakrabarty
1980 · United States
Ananda Chakrabarty created a genetically modified bacterium that could eat oil spills. The USPTO rejected his patent — you can't patent a living thing. The Supreme Court disagreed 5–4: "anything under the sun made by man" is patentable. This decision unleashed the biotechnology industry and enabled patents on genes, GMOs, and later, human cell lines.
⚡ Impact: Made biotech investment possible. Directly led to the $2.5T global biotech industry — and decades of gene patent controversy.
🔒 Trade Secret · Federal Circuit
Waymo v. Uber
2018 · United States
Anthony Levandowski downloaded 14,000 confidential files from Waymo (Google's self-driving car unit) before leaving to found a startup acquired by Uber. Waymo sued. The case settled for $245M in Uber equity. Levandowski was later convicted of trade secret theft and sentenced to prison — later pardoned by President Trump. The clearest modern example of human capital and IP colliding.
⚡ Impact: Redefined trade secret theft risk in tech. Made "brain drain" between competitors a legal minefield.
📱 Patent · District Court
Apple v. Samsung
2011–2018 · Global
Apple sued Samsung for $2.5 billion, claiming Samsung copied the iPhone's look and feel. The litigation sprawled across 10 countries over 7 years, with over 50 separate lawsuits. Final US verdict: Samsung owed $539M. The case exposed the absurdity of "design patent" law — Samsung was partly found liable for the rounded corners of a rectangle.
⚡ Impact: Made design patents a serious weapon. Triggered global debates about whether IP law was strangling smartphone innovation.
📻 Copyright · 9th Circuit
Authors Guild v. Google
2015 · United States
Google scanned 20+ million books without permission to build Google Books. Authors sued. The 2nd Circuit ruled Google's actions were fair use — the digitization served a "transformative" public interest (search and discovery) without substituting for the original works. A landmark for the internet age: copying at massive scale can be legal if the purpose is transformative.
⚡ Impact: Established "transformation" as the key fair use test — the legal foundation now being tested by generative AI training.
🤖 Copyright · USPTO / Courts
Thaler v. Vidal (DABUS)
2022 · US & Global
Stephen Thaler built an AI called DABUS that he claimed invented two products autonomously. He applied for patents listing DABUS as the inventor. Courts in the US, UK, EU, and Australia all ruled the same way: only a human can be an inventor. No human = no patent. The AI's output was unprotectable. The question of AI-generated IP has yet to be definitively resolved anywhere on Earth.
⚡ Impact: Established the "human authorship" requirement as the global baseline for IP protection — now the central battleground of AI law.
IV
Chapter Four
AI & the IP Crisis

Generative AI has created the most significant stress test in the history of intellectual property law. Five unresolved tensions are reshaping the field right now — and none of them have clear answers.

Training Data Consent
AI models trained on billions of copyrighted works without permission or payment. Is this fair use? The New York Times vs. OpenAI case could be the Napster moment for generative AI.
Output Ownership
If AI writes your novel, who owns it? Current law says: you can copyright your prompts and edits, but not the AI's contribution. How much human involvement is "enough"?
Style vs. Expression
Copyright protects expression, not style. You can't copyright "painting like Monet." But when AI generates thousands of Monet-style images, does that destroy the value of the style itself?
Model Weights as Trade Secrets
OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic treat their trained models as trade secrets. But unlike Coca-Cola's formula, model weights encode millions of human creative works. Can you secrete others' expression?
AI as Inventor
As AI systems autonomously design new drugs, materials, and devices — with no human "inventor" in the traditional sense — the patent system faces its deepest identity crisis since 1790.
